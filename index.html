The Conflict

Commodity traders read satellite NDVI like a stock ticker. USDA economists use it to flag harvest shortfalls months before any bag of cereal gets a new price tag. The same averaging blind spot that hides a county's drought also distorts every forecast built on top of it.

But state averages tell a comforting fiction.

When you paint an entire state a single shade of green, you erase the real story. Western Kansas is a desert. Eastern Kansas is humid. A single number cannot describe both — yet that is exactly what most climate dashboards show you.

The Lifecycle

Every crop draws its own ring.

Read the year clockwise from January. The further the line from center, the greener the land. Iowa explodes upward as corn matures in July, Kansas climbs steadily and stays high through summer, while Texas peaks early in May and then dips as the brutal heat sets in.

A note on overlapping rings

Each ring traces one state's monthly NDVI at its exact value — no smoothing or offsetting. Where two rings run close together, the states genuinely reached similar greenness that month. That proximity is itself the signal, not noise. Hover any dot to read its exact NDVI.

The Relationships

Heat and rain do not love crops equally.

Each dot is one state in one month. The line connecting points is an indicator of time sequence from January to December. Watch what happens when you isolate a single crop — the universal-looking relationship between climate and vegetation fractures into three very different stories.

A note on overlapping dots

Every labelled bubble is placed at the exact (x, y) of its underlying value — no jittering, no displacement. When two months share nearly identical climate, their dots overlap on purpose. That proximity is the finding: it shows the months were climatologically interchangeable. Hover any bubble to read its exact NDVI, temperature, and precipitation.

A Hidden Variable

Crops feel the night too.

Plants respire after dark. The wider the day-night gap in land surface temperature — the "skin" temperature MODIS reads directly off the soil and canopy, not air — the more energy crops burn just to stay alive, energy that should have gone into building grain or fiber. Below, each panel splits day-time LST from night-time LST for every month of 2023.

So what did we learn?

Three findings, one warning.

01

State averages can lie by 0.6 NDVI.

In April 2023, eastern Texas counties hit a near-rainforest 0.79 while western Texas counties never break 0.15. Reporting a single state value masks a 0.64 gap inside one state — wider than any difference between Iowa, Kansas, and Texas as wholes.

02

Temperature alone does not explain greenness.

Texas is hotter than Iowa every month — yet Iowa's NDVI peaks higher. Crop choice, the timing of rain, and even the night-time chill matter as much as raw daytime heat. The same 30 °C means thriving corn in well-watered Iowa and moisture-starved cotton across West Texas.

03

The night tells the other half.

Texas night-time LST stays above 20 °C from June through September, denying cotton the cool hours plants use to recover. Iowa's nights, by contrast, average 18 °C even in July — giving corn overnight respite that satellites quantify in vivid detail.

! The warning

Time to retire the state average.

The 0.64 NDVI gap we measured inside a single Texas in April 2023 is not an outlier — it is what every farm state looks like once you stop averaging. As climate change widens the wettest-to-driest divide inside each state, that gap will only deepen. State-level reporting was an approximation born of measurement limits decades ago. Those limits no longer exist.

What we built · What was hard

What we have done

  • Pulled MODIS NDVI (MOD13A2), LST day & night (MOD11A2), and CHIRPS precipitation for every county in Iowa, Kansas, and Texas for the full 2023 calendar year — all aggregated to monthly means inside Google Earth Engine.
  • Built an eight-section scrollytelling page in D3.js, anchored by six interactive visualisations: a US intro map, a state-vs-county reveal, a 458-county time machine with sparkline hover, a radial crop calendar, paired scatter plots with crop-stage annotations, and a day-vs-night LST chart.
  • Added a site-wide °C ↔ °F toggle so American and international readers can each read in their native unit — every choropleth tick, axis label, tooltip, and inline number on the page re-renders in place, without a page reload.
  • Authored an And-But-Therefore narrative connecting satellite observation to crop-specific lifecycle knowledge.

What was the most challenging

  • Designing interactions that reveal patterns a static plot cannot — especially the per-county sparkline-on-hover, which lets readers compare one county's full-year trajectory against the regional choropleth without leaving the page.
  • Keeping three side-by-side maps (Iowa, Kansas, Texas) visually balanced when Texas is nearly 5× larger than Iowa: each map is independently fit to its own bounds while sharing one global color scale.
  • Implementing the °C/°F toggle correctly — absolute temperatures need the +32 offset (0 °C = 32 °F) but temperature differences only scale (a 21 °C gap is 38 °F, not 70 °F). The two cases live in separate API functions so a tooltip's Day, Night, and Gap rows convert independently without us silently corrupting any of them.
  • Writing prose that turns satellite numbers into a story a non-specialist can actually feel.
